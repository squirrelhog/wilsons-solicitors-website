What is a Wrongful Death Claim?

A wrongful death claim arises when a person dies as a result of another party's negligent, reckless, or intentional conduct. These claims allow certain family members and dependents to seek compensation for the losses they have suffered due to the death of their loved one.

Common Causes of Wrongful Death:

  • Motor Vehicle Accidents - Car, motorcycle, truck, and pedestrian accidents caused by negligent drivers
  • Medical Negligence - Death resulting from medical malpractice, surgical errors, or misdiagnosis
  • Workplace Accidents - Fatal injuries at work due to unsafe conditions or inadequate safety measures
  • Defective Products - Deaths caused by dangerous or defective consumer products
  • Nursing Home Neglect - Death resulting from substandard care in aged care facilities
  • Premises Liability - Fatal accidents on dangerous or poorly maintained properties
  • Construction Accidents - Deaths on construction sites due to safety violations
  • Assault and Violence - Homicide or violent acts by third parties
  • Aviation and Maritime Accidents - Fatal crashes and accidents in air or water transport
  • Public Transport Accidents - Deaths involving buses, trains, or other public transport

Who Can Bring a Wrongful Death Claim?

Under NSW law, wrongful death claims can be pursued by specific family members and dependents.

Eligible Claimants Include:

  • Spouse or De Facto Partner - Including same-sex partners
  • Children - Biological, adopted, and step-children
  • Parents - Including adoptive and step-parents
  • Siblings - Brothers and sisters of the deceased
  • Grandparents and Grandchildren - In certain circumstances
  • Financial Dependents - Anyone who was financially dependent on the deceased
  • Members of the Household - Those who lived with and were cared for by the deceased

Estate Claims

The executor or administrator of the deceased's estate can also bring claims on behalf of the estate for:

  • Medical and funeral expenses
  • Loss of estate value (earnings the deceased would have contributed to the estate)
  • Pain and suffering before death

Types of Compensation Available

Wrongful death claims can provide compensation for both economic and non-economic losses.

Economic Damages:

  • Loss of Financial Support - Income and financial contributions the deceased would have provided
  • Loss of Superannuation Benefits - Future superannuation the deceased would have accrued
  • Medical Expenses - Costs of medical treatment prior to death
  • Funeral and Burial Costs - Reasonable funeral expenses
  • Loss of Services - Value of household services and childcare the deceased provided
  • Loss of Inheritance - Expected inheritance from the deceased's future earnings
  • Out-of-Pocket Expenses - Travel, accommodation, and other expenses related to the death

Non-Economic Damages:

  • Loss of Care and Companionship - Compensation for the relationship with the deceased
  • Loss of Guidance and Support - Particularly important for children who lost parents
  • Grief and Mental Anguish - Emotional distress and suffering
  • Loss of Consortium - Loss of intimate relationship for spouses

Solatium (Bereavement) Damages:

NSW law provides for statutory payments to certain family members for grief and loss:

  • Spouses and de facto partners
  • Parents who lost children
  • Children who lost parents

Establishing a Wrongful Death Claim

To succeed in a wrongful death claim, we must establish:

Key Elements:

  • Duty of Care - The defendant owed a duty of care to the deceased
  • Breach of Duty - The defendant breached that duty through negligence or wrongful conduct
  • Causation - The breach directly caused the death
  • Damages - You suffered losses as a result of the death
  • Relationship - You have standing as an eligible claimant

Evidence We Gather:

  • Police reports and accident investigations
  • Medical records and autopsy reports
  • Expert witness testimony (medical, accident reconstruction, economic)
  • Witness statements from those who saw what happened
  • Employment and financial records of the deceased
  • Documentation of the relationship with the deceased
  • Evidence of financial dependency and losses
  • Photographs, videos, and physical evidence

Time Limits for Wrongful Death Claims

Critical: Strict time limits apply to wrongful death claims in NSW.

Key Limitation Periods:

  • General Rule: 3 years from the date of death
  • Motor Vehicle Accidents: Claims must be notified within 6 months; court proceedings within 3 years
  • Workers Compensation Deaths: Specific time limits apply for different types of claims
  • Public Liability: 3 years from date of death
  • Medical Negligence: 3 years from when you became aware (or should have become aware) of the negligence

Important: Extensions may be available in limited circumstances, but it's essential to seek legal advice as soon as possible after the death to protect your rights.

Special Considerations

Criminal Proceedings and Coronial Inquests

Wrongful death claims are separate from criminal proceedings or coronial inquests:

  • Criminal charges may be laid against the responsible party
  • Coroner's inquests investigate the cause and circumstances of death
  • Civil wrongful death claims seek financial compensation for families
  • We coordinate with criminal and coronial processes while pursuing your claim
  • Evidence from criminal trials and inquests can support civil claims

Multiple Claimants

When multiple family members have claims:

  • We coordinate claims to avoid duplication and maximize recovery
  • Courts apportion damages among eligible claimants
  • We ensure fair distribution of compensation
  • We represent multiple family members where appropriate

Dependency Claims for Children

Special attention is given to claims by or on behalf of children:

  • Calculating loss of financial support until maturity
  • Valuing loss of parental guidance and care
  • Protecting children's interests in settlements
  • Court approval required for settlements involving minors
  • Structured settlements to provide long-term security

Common Defendants in Wrongful Death Claims

Wrongful death claims may be brought against various parties:

  • Individual Drivers - Negligent motorists who caused fatal accidents
  • Employers - Companies responsible for workplace safety failures
  • Healthcare Providers - Doctors, hospitals, and medical facilities
  • Product Manufacturers - Companies that made defective products
  • Property Owners - Owners of dangerous premises
  • Government Entities - Councils, state agencies, and public authorities
  • Aged Care Facilities - Nursing homes and residential care providers
  • Construction Companies - Builders and contractors responsible for site safety
  • Insurance Companies - Representing liable parties
